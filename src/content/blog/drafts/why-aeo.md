# Of all the AI topics, why AEO?

There's a version of this post that leads with a market opportunity statistic. I'm not going to write that post.

This one is more personal, and probably less useful to you if you landed here looking for a technical framework. That's okay. The other posts have those. This one is about why I keep coming back to this specific corner of the AI conversation when there are dozens of other corners to stand in.

## How we searched before we searched

My oldest siblings were born in the 1980s. I grew up hearing about what writing a research paper actually looked like for them: the library, the card catalog, the encyclopedia set in the living room that my parents bought on an installment plan. If you needed to know something, you either knew it, knew someone who knew it, or made a physical trip to a building that stored it. The information existed. Getting to it was the work.

By the time I was writing papers, that had started to shift. Early search engines were a revelation and also, in retrospect, kind of a disaster. Lycos. AltaVista. Ask Jeeves, which in its own clunky way was ahead of its time. Google showed up and made everything feel like it was supposed to be easy, but the actual experience of doing research still required a lot of you. You'd get a list of blue links, open fifteen tabs, triangulate across sources, take notes, and synthesize something yourself. The library card catalog was gone but the work of thinking through sources wasn't. You were still the analyst.

Then Wikipedia happened and suddenly a reasonable first draft of almost any answer was one search away. Teachers hated this. They were right to be suspicious of it in some ways and completely wrong about why.

Now I watch kids in high school ask ChatGPT to explain something and receive a synthesized, conversational, contextually aware answer in seconds. Not a list of links. Not raw material to process. An answer. The reading and the synthesizing that used to be your job? Already done.

That three-generation arc sits with me. It's not just a story about technology getting faster. It's a story about where the cognitive work of learning lives.

## The experience of an expert

There's a specific feeling I'm chasing when I'm trying to understand something new. It's not the feeling of finding a useful article. It's the feeling of getting twenty minutes with someone who has spent years on a problem and is willing to just... talk it through with you.

You know the feeling. You ask a question and instead of getting an answer, you get context. Then another question back at you, to figure out what you actually need. Then a reframe that makes the whole thing land differently than it did before. Then, maybe, the answer, which now makes sense because of the three things that came before it.

That conversation is rarer than it should be. Access to that kind of expertise has always been unevenly distributed. If you happened to know the right person, or go to the right school, or work in the right building, you could get those conversations fairly regularly. If you didn't, you were back to the library.

When I first started using AI tools seriously, this was the thing that struck me. Not the speed. Not the breadth of knowledge. The conversational structure. The way a good prompt gets you something closer to that expert conversation than anything a list of links ever did. You can ask a follow-up. You can say "that's not quite what I meant." You can push back and watch the response adjust.

AI search is that experience applied to the information layer of the internet. And that is genuinely new.

## Why AEO specifically

There are a lot of legitimate places to focus attention in the AI space. Model architecture. Safety. Regulation. Enterprise tooling. I find almost all of it interesting. But AEO is where I keep returning because it sits at the intersection of two things I care about: how information gets communicated, and who gets access to it.

SEO was always partially about this. If you wrote genuinely useful content and structured it well, search engines would help people find it. AEO takes that further. The way AI platforms synthesize information means that the question of "whose content gets surfaced" is no longer just a ranking problem. It's closer to a credibility and communication problem. Content that clearly answers questions, from sources that have built legitimate authority on a topic, gets cited. That's a different game, and I think it's a better game.

The technical mechanics are interesting. The vector space, the RAG pipelines, the chunking behavior. I've written about all of that. But underneath the mechanics is a simpler question: what does it mean for a piece of writing to be genuinely useful to someone asking a question? AEO forces that question in a way that I find healthy.

I also think we're in a genuinely formative moment. The habits and patterns being set right now, both by the platforms and by the people creating content for them, are going to shape what AI search looks like for a long time. Getting in early isn't about gaming anything. It's about being part of figuring out what good looks like.

## The part where I let myself be a little naive

I want to end this somewhere more optimistic than "it's a good time to start an AEO practice," because that feels like burying the actual point.

The diaspora of human knowledge is kind of staggering to think about. There are things that exist only in recordings that haven't been digitized. There are books that exist in one library in one city. There are conversations that happened in offices or kitchens or on park benches that contained ideas that never got written down. There are research papers locked behind paywalls that only people at well-funded institutions can read. The information isn't gone. Getting to it is still the work, and for most people, the work is prohibitive.

AI search, as it currently exists, is a partial solution to this. A significant one, but partial. The models are expensive to run. The energy costs are real. The access is uneven in ways that track pretty closely to existing inequalities in who has reliable internet, who has time to learn new tools, and who has a reason to trust systems that haven't always worked in their favor.

But here's the thing I keep coming back to: energy infrastructure is changing. Renewable capacity is being built at a scale that would have been hard to predict even five years ago. And the people building AI infrastructure are, at least sometimes, thinking about how to build on top of it sustainably from the start rather than retrofitting later.

If the energy problem gets solved at the infrastructure level (and I think it will, slower than the optimists say and faster than the pessimists expect), then the cost barrier starts to fall. And if the cost barrier falls far enough, the possibility that opens up is something like a universal, free, public super-library. Not a list of links. Not a card catalog. An actual answer, from synthesized access to the full scope of what human beings have written, recorded, typed, and said, available to anyone with a connection.

That sounds like science fiction. It also sounds like where this is pointing, if enough things go roughly right.

AEO is, for me, a small piece of getting ready for that. Understanding how to communicate clearly enough that an AI can use your content. Understanding how authoritative signals get built. Understanding where this is heading, so that when the scale shifts, we know what we're doing.

I find it hard to stay detached from that. Some topics you study. Some topics you follow because you can feel something significant happening and you want to be paying attention when it does.

This is the second kind.
